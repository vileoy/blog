Behler-Parrinello Symmetry Functions

Many assignments urgently need to be solved in computational chemistry is about modeling the relationship between molecular structure and its property, such as potential energy prediction and binding affinity prediction. In contrast to the ab initio tasks, neural network models that using the Cartesian coordinates of the atoms as an input always suffer from the problems of variance with respect to rotations, translations, and permutations of identical atoms. To satisfy the invariance requirements, many descriptors have been introduced in place of coordinates. And Behler-Parrinello symmetry functions is a type of them describing the correlations between particles within the environment.

Introduction

The Behler-Parrinello symmetry functions was first introduced by Behler and Parrinello for neural network potentials. Its success in electron energy approximation renders it a good representation of an atomic environment that is invariant to the translation, rotational, reflection, and permutation symmetries. And now, some works have resorted to it for modeling protein-ligand interactions, e.g. ACNN, graphDelta.

Formulation

Herein, we start the discussion of the symmetry functions representing a local atomic environment. First of all, it is natural to prioritize local environment by a cut-off function \(f_{c} (r_{ij})\) which smoothly decreases the weights of atoms outside the proximal environment and assigns the zero weight for atoms outside the cut-off distance. \[ f_{\text{c}} (r_{ij}) = \left\{\begin{array}{ll} \tanh^{3}\left(1 - \frac{r_{ij}}{r_{\text{c}}}\right) & \text{for}\ r_{ij} \leq r_{\text{c}} \\ 0 & \text{for}\ r_{ij} > r_{\text{c}} \end{array}\right. \] Behler-Parrinello symmetry functions contain forms which depend only on the distance to the neighboring atoms and forms which are based on angles formed by all atom pairs in the environment and the central atom as an angle vertex. All these symmetry functions together describe the correlations between atoms in a neighborhood of a central atom with index \(i\).

Pair Correlations

The first functional forms, called \(G^{2}\), provide information about pair correlations between the atoms. And the common choice is \[ G_{i}^{2} = \sum_{j \neq i}^{\text{all}} \mathrm{e}^{-\eta(r_{ij} - r_{\text{s}})^{2}} f_{\text{c}}(r_{ij}) \] where the parameters \(\eta\) and \(r_{\text{s}}\) control the width and the position of the Gaussian with respect to the central atom, respectively. The sum is over all neighboring atoms being closer than \(r_{\text{c}}\).

Angular Correlations

The second type of symmetry functions, called \(G^{3}\), provides information about angular correlations and commonly has the form \[ G_{i}^{3} = 2^{1-\zeta} \sum_{j,k \neq i}^{\text{all}} (1 + \lambda \cos \theta_{ijk})^{\zeta} \mathrm{e}^{-\eta(r_{ij}^{2} + r_{jk}^{2} + r_{ik}^{2})} f_{\text{c}}(r_{ij}) f_{\text{c}}(r_{ik}) f_{\text{c}}(r_{jk}) \] where \(\zeta\), \(\eta\), and \(\lambda\) are the three parameters that determine the shape of this type of symmetry function. The indices \(j\) and \(k\) run over all the atoms in the neighbourhood of the tagged atom \(i\).

The parameter \(\lambda\) can have the values \(+1\) and \(-1\) shifting the maxima of the cosine function to \(\theta_{ijk} = 0\) and \(θ_{ijk} = \pi\), respectively.

  • \(\lambda = +1\)
  • \(\lambda = -1\)
